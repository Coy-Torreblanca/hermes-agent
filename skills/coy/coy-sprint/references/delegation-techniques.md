# Delegation Techniques — Work Context

Patterns Coy has used successfully to protect engineering time while still moving non-engineering work forward.

## Technique 1: Approval Gate ≠ Ownership

**Scenario:** Someone creates a deliverable (form, doc, process) and needs your approval. The meeting is scheduled for 30 minutes.

**The play:**
1. **Cap the time** — "I can give you 10 minutes, not 30." Approve or give fixes and move on.
2. **Approve as a gate, not an owner** — You're checking that it's correct, not taking responsibility for it going forward.
3. **Push ownership off** — Ask "who will be the registered owner for this?" Don't let silence make you the default. Someone else will often volunteer.
4. **Result:** Clean handoff, no lingering maintenance burden, no future meetings about this item.

**Why it works:** Being the approver doesn't mean you're the owner. Separating these roles is a permanent win — you're not on the hook for updates, maintenance, or future meetings.

**Example (2026-05-06):** Anil built a snow form and wanted Coy's approval in a 30-min meeting. Coy capped it to 10 min, approved the form, then explicitly pushed back on being the registered owner. Someone else volunteered. Clean exit.

## Technique 2: Authority-Naming

**Scenario:** Someone is pushing back or slow-rolling a request you need done.

**The play:** Name the authority behind the request to overcome resistance. "Sean said you'd be good for this." 

This works because it (a) signals the request has backing, (b) implies follow-up will happen if it stalls, and (c) frames it as a vote of confidence rather than an order.

## Technique 3: Wording, Not Debate

**Scenario:** Someone raises a late objection to something you've already approved, applying scrutiny they didn't apply to others.

**The play:** Don't debate the objection. Ask for the specific wording change they want. "Send me the updated wording and I'll call from there." This:
- Keeps it concrete (no scope creep)
- Puts the work back on them
- Avoids getting drawn into an argument about standards

**Example (2026-05-06):** Chuck pushed back on Coy's snow form approval, raising a concern about service IDs he hadn't flagged with the application team. Coy told him to send the updated wording and will call to resolve.

## Anti-Pattern: Meeting Snowball

> "I'm wary of 'meeting snowball' — scope creep from meetings."

A 30-minute meeting about a delegated task is the snowball forming. Kill it early: cap the time, approve and exit, push ownership off.
